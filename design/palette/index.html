Palette Design System

Background

Over the past 3 years, the Artsy design team has taken various attempts at developing a cohesive design system to enable quicker and more consistent iteration across our products. Without a consistent approach, these initiatives have been limited in scope to specific product areas and have failed to gain traction within the organization.

Working without dedicated design systems resources, we knew we must focus on creating a system that could be developed in a distributed manner and whose work could be incorporated into existing product teams work as much as possible.

Systems audit

Assessing our existing implementation of common UI and UX patterns revealed a high level of inconsistency in both the design treatment and technical implementation of functionally similar components.

As our codebase moved more and more into React, our usage of components continued to be team specific, and lacked a cohesive strategy. These divergent approachs resulted in a large amount of overhead in developing new experiences and iterating on current products.

Designing process

With the goal of creating a system that was easily consumable by a variety of teams working both on design and engineering, we focused our attention on first articulating a lightweight and easily replicable process for any potential contributors. The result of this was the formation of a small task force, meeting on a biweekly basis, and robust documentation for all aspects of the design systems development process.

Our documentation was created to ensure a consistent style and framework for all additions to our design system. This was especially important for our team as both the engineering and design team scaled without dedicated resources focused on the health and continued application of these systems.

System heirarchy

The most fundamental problem for our task force to address was the question of what should be included in a cohesive design system. Creating a hierarchical vocabularly allowed us to assess all potential additions to the Palette code base based on their specificity of application and their scalability accross our product experiences. All additions to this system should be useful across a many of our products, working in a wide range contexts and for a variety of users.

Components: typography

After an inital round of standardization of our type sizes and consolidation of their implementation we began developing an additional layer of typographic variables. The semantic naming of these variable created more clarity in how they should be applied. Additionally, these variables incorporated responsive logic allowing us to more easily scale type relationships across our breakpoints.

Throughout this process, it was cruicial to continually test these variables accross our key product surfaces to ensure its functionality.

Components: quick inputs

Our quick edit input were created to capture information in contexts where experiences must be as lightweight as possible. Used prominitely in our log in and sign up forms, these elements are meant to be as condensed as possible while retaining their clairity and functionality.

Components: modals

Modals are used across our platform for quick actions and to surface important contextual information. The new modal compontent in Palette was created with two size variations, and a number of standardized treatment options for CTAs, forms, titles and other commonly used UI.

Components: data visualization

An initial bar chart component was initially created when we began surfacing data to users both on the front end of the site and in our partner-facing CMS. Soon after, additional visualization options were added to support a wider variety of data types.

Moving forward

As our design system is increasingly adopted across our product areas, we have continued to emphasis the importance of documentation of usage, and guidelines, creating a centralized, structured home for this work to live. This work will continue to evolve and be refined as we increase useage and find the boundries of our current systems.
